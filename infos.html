a. Mental Health & Wellness

Mental health and wellness involves your emotional, psychological, and social well-being. It affects how you think, feel, and act. It's about maintaining a positive state of mind, managing stress, and building resilience.

Why does it matter?

College can be a stressful time and taking care of your mental health is just as important as taking care of your physical health. Prioritizing your mental well-being can help you succeed academically and experience college better.

Here are the aspects involved in Mental health and wellness:

Self-Care is the practice of individuals managing their health using available knowledge and information. It is a decision-making process that empowers individuals to take care of their health efficiently while collaborating with health and social care professionals as needed. For college students, self-care is especially important due to the pressures of academic demands, social life, and other stressors. Components of Self-Care are:

  1. Physical Self-Care

    Physical activity is important because it boosts your mood, relieves stress, and enhances concentration. Some examples are:

    • Exercise Regularly - Join a campus gym, take regular walks around campus, or try recreational clubs to make exercise more fun!
    • Prioritize Sleep - Sleep is essential for memory retention, cognitive functions, and your overall health. Set a consistent sleep schedule, avoid caffeine or electronics. If you're pulling an all-nighter, take a power nap for a few minutes to recharge.
    • Eat Nourishing Meals - Nutrition plays a huge role in maintaining energy levels and focus. Keep healthy snacks on hand, plan easy meals, stay hydrated and carry a water bottle.

  2. Emotional Self-Care

    Understanding and processing your feelings helps prevent emotional burnout. Some tips are to journal your thoughts regularly to reflect on what you're feeling; Talk to someone you trust, whether it's a friend or a counselor; and Practice self-compassion.

    • Seek Support - Social and professional support systems are crucial for emotional stability. Take advantage of campus mental health resources like counseling services. Don't hesitate to reach out to friends if you're feeling stressed or down. Join organizations that interest you to build a supportive community.

  3. Mental Self-Care

    College can be stressful, but managing stress is essential for academic success and overall well-being. Some tips are to practice relaxation techniques like deep breathing, meditation, or yoga; Use time management tools to stay on top of deadlines; Break your assignments into smaller, manageable tasks to avoid feeling overwhelmed.

    • Stay Mentally Engaged - Intellectual stimulation helps keep your mind sharp and motivated. Attend study groups or engage in thought-provoking discussions. Read beyond your textbooks and explore new interests to keep your mind curious. Take breaks to avoid mental fatigue and use a study technique that works for you.

  4. Spiritual Self-Care

    Spiritual self-care helps you stay grounded and motivated. Some tips are to spend time in nature to reconnect with yourself and the world around you; Practice meditation to reduce stress and gain clarity; Reflect on your goals and values to stay focused on what's important.

    • Create a routine for reflection - Regular reflection helps you manage your thoughts and emotions. Start your day with a brief moment of affirmation. Attend religious services if that aligns with your beliefs.

  5. Social Self-Care

    Social connections provide emotional support, joy, and a sense of belonging. Make time for friends, whether it's a weekly hangout or study session. Join student organizations, clubs, or volunteer groups to meet new people, and celebrate achievements with others whether its small or big.

    • Set Boundaries - Setting boundaries helps protect your energy and time. Learn to say no when you're feeling overwhelmed with commitments. Communicate your limits with roommates, classmates, and friends. Protect your study time and personal space.

Mindfulness is all about living in the now. It's about paying attention to your thoughts, feelings, and body sensations without judgment. Through mindfulness, you can gain clarity, reduce stress, and increase your focus. Mindfulness practices you can start today:

  • Mindful Breathing - Pay attention to the sensation of your breath as it enters and leaves your body.
  • Mindful Walking - Focus on the sensation of your feet touching the ground as you walk.
  • Mindful Eating - Pay attention to the taste, texture, and smell of your food and savour each bite.
  • Body Scan Meditation - Bring your attention to different parts of your body while noticing sensations.

Sleep Hygiene and its Role in Mental Health

What is Sleep Hygiene?

Sleep hygiene is about having healthy habits that promote consistent and quality sleep. This includes maintaining a consistent sleep schedule and creating a restful environment.Sleep is like a reset button for our brain. Getting a good rest, ensures that we wake up feeling refreshed, focused and ready to take on the day. Have you ever tried pulling an all-nighter and then functioning like a normal person the next day? Yeah it doesn't work like that. Getting a good sleep helps us to be sharp, helps you manage stress and improve your mood. Having poor sleep hygiene can negatively affect both sleep quality and quantity.

This is not just about how many hours you sleep but it is also about how well you sleep. Skipping sleep to give yourself more study time sounds like a small thing, but the truth is: Bad sleep = bad everything. A good sleep hygiene means having both a bedroom environment and daily routine that promotes consistent and uninterrupted sleep.

When pulling an all-nighter, have you ever felt 10x more stressful the next day? That is because of your poor sleep that messes up your brain's ability to regulate emotions. It has been found that sleep disruptions alter brain chemistry, which can lead to the development of mental health disorder.

Is your sleep hygiene in trouble?

  1. Tossing and turning all night. Staring at the ceiling or just lying in bed. If it takes you more than 30 minutes to fall asleep, that's a red flag!
  2. Daytime sleepiness.If you feel like you're constantly fighting to stay awake during the day, this means that your body isn’t getting enough sleep that it needs.
  3. Waking up feeling totally tired.This also happens when your sleep cycle is disrupted, this means that you don’t get enough sleep.
  4. itting snooze more than 10 times. Just five more minutes... If you’re struggling to wake up, this means that your body is not properly refreshed. This could mean that you sleep or take a rest at the wrong hour or you just don’t get a quality sleep.
  5. You rely on caffeine and naps to function.If drinking coffee, energy drinks and taking a power nap is the only way for you to function, it’s a sign that your sleep is not doing its job.
  6. You feel moody, anxious or stuck in a brain fog.Taking a proper sleep is important for emotional regulation. A poor sleep doesn't just tire you out, this also messes with your emotion, mental health and focus. If you ever feel easily irritated or struggle to concentrate, the reason behind this could be lack of sleep.
If these seem familiar to you, then don’t worry we can help you fix your sleep habit. Just keep reading to find out!

How can you improve your sleep hygiene?

  1. Set your sleep schedule. Go to bed and wake up at the same time everyday. This will help in regulating your body clock, making it easier for you to fall asleep and wake up.
  2. Follow a nightly routine. Keep the routine consistent, this will help your body to recognize that it’s bedtime when you start the routine. Your routine can include whatever that makes you feel relaxed, here are some examples:
    1. Taking a warm bath. The water from the shower can be relaxing but the drop in the body temperature as you cool down can help you feel sleepy.
    2. Try doing stretches to help your muscles relax and release some tension.
    3. You can also try meditating to help your mind and body to be calm.
    4. Give yourself at least 30 minutes to read a book.
  3. Refrain from using digital devices one to two hours before bedtime. These devices emit blue light, this reduces the melatonin levels in your body. It tricks your brain that it’s still daytime, which makes it hard for you to fall asleep.
  4. Exercise regularly. You can exercise as little as 30 minutes a day. You can try aerobic exercises to improve your sleep quality and overall health. You can do it indoors or even outdoors. Remember to avoid exercising when it's near your bedtime.
  5. Limit your caffeine intake. The effects of caffeine inside your body can last for 3-7 hours after consuming it. The afternoon coffee you took might keep you up at night.
  6. Make sure that your sleep environment works for you. Your bedroom should make you feel comfortable and relaxed. Too much noise or a bright environment can make it hard for you to fully rest.
  7. Limit napping. Naps are amazing to recharge yourself for a few minutes. But a long and late nap can mess up your body clock. If you need to take a nap you should keep it to 20 minutes or less.
  8. Minimize light. Bright lights can trick your brain and keep you awake. When it’s dark tour brain produces melatonin, this is a hormone which makes you feel sleepy. But when you’re exposed to bright light, your brain will think that it's still daytime. If you’re someone who can’t sleep without any lights, you can try switching to a warm-dim light.
  9. Don’t eat heavy meals before going to bed. Eating heavy meals before bedtime can affect you negatively. Consume meals at least three hours before your bedtime.
By following these tips, we hope that you can wake up feeling more energized, focused and ready to take on your day!

Do you ever feel like there's just not enough time in the day? What is Procrastination?

The habit of delaying or postponing tasks is what we call Procrastination. It's putting off tasks which need to be done. It is the failure in self-regulation that can lead to acting irrationally. We delay things off, even when we know that we should be doing them. For example, instead of starting your essay that is due tomorrow, you decided to open Tiktok for a quick scroll—an hour later you find yourself panicking at midnight with nothing but a blank page.

Why do we procrastinate?

Procrastination is more than a lack of time management skills. The tendency to procrastinate often comes from deeper issues. We often procrastinate when we find tasks most difficult, unpleasant or boring. Some common reasons for procrastination are the following:

  • Feeling bored: There are times when you find an activity or a task boring and unpleasant, which is why you're far more likely to put it off until later. Our brain naturally looks for something more exciting. This is why scrolling through TikTok seems way more appealing than writing a research paper.
  • Feeling overwhelmed: There are times where tasks feel so overwhelming and complicated that we just end up avoiding it altogether. This feeling can be linked to a lack of energy, trying to do multiple things at once or being a perfectionist.
  • Avoiding unpleasant feelings: Humans tend to avoid things that make them feel bad. The pleasure-pain principle by Sigmund Freud suggests that we are more motivated to obtain pleasure and avoid pain. This shows that we humans tend to do things that give us instant satisfaction and gratification rather than the things that we find difficult, overwhelming or unpleasant.
  • Fearing failure: When we fear making mistakes we tend to avoid doing any important tasks. The more anxiety we feel about a task, the more likely we'll put it off later.
  • Distraction: Distractions in our surroundings prevents us from being able to focus on the task at hand.
  • Lacking of belief in your abilities: This is another reason why we tend to procrastinate. If you lack faith in your abilities, you're more likely to put it off. It is important for you to believe in yourself so you can carry out tasks efficiently.
  • Adhering to bad habits: Habits are hard to break and procrastination is considered as a habit. If you let yourself get used to waiting until the last minute to do your tasks, your brain will get used to keep on doing that.

How to overcome Procrastination?
STEP 1: Know yourself and find out 🕵️‍♂️
Now that you know what's holding you back, you need to figure out how you work best. For this step imagine yourself as a detective investigating your own life.

  1. Spot the problem: To overcome procrastination you need to understand the reasons why you procrastinate . To come up with an effective solution, you need to really understand the root of the problem. Self-awareness and self-knowledge is the key for you to figure out how to put an end to this bad habit.
  2. Where does your time really go? Conduct a time audit. This will help you track how you spend your day. You might think that you don't have enough time, but in reality you just managed it poorly. By conducting a time audit you will be able to expose hidden time-wasters. Track everything you do for a full day.
  3. Figure out your biggest time wasters: We have different time wasters which consume our valuable time. It is important for you to be aware of what those time wasters are. Scrolling through the internet, watching TV, and playing online games are the well known time wasters. Once you know the time wasters that consume your time, you can use the right strategies to fight back.
  4. Find your most productive time: Some people work best at night, while others are productive at 5 am. By knowing what your best work time will help you create a schedule for tasks. Are you a Morning person? Or do you prefer to work during the evenings? Or maybe you’re a midday warrior? Take advantage of this energy and schedule your time as close to it as possible.

STEP 2: Master your time ⏰

  1. The Eisenhower Matrix: This is a technique for organizing your tasks into four separate quadrants based on urgency and importance.
    2. How it works:
    1. Important and Urgent - Do it right away, these are tasks that has pressing deadlines.
    2. Important but not Urgent - Decide when to do it, these tasks matter but they don't have an immediate deadline. Schedule this ahead of time so you won't have to do it last minute.
    3. Not important but urgent - Delegate, these are tasks that is urgent but does not require you
    4. Not important and not urgent - Delete, these are tasks that don't add any value and just consume your time.
  2. Eat the Frog:This technique is developed by Brian Tracey. It is inspired from the quote misattributed to Mark Twain which says “if you eat a live frog first thing in the morning, nothing worse will happen to you for the rest of the day”. This method involves starting your day by doing the most difficult task and getting them out of the way.
    3. How it works:
    1. Set a clear goal
    2. Write it down
    3. Compile a list of tasks that you need to accomplish
    4. Organize the list in order of priority. The “frogs” are the most important and most difficult tasks.
    5. Take action
  3. Pomodoro Technique: This technique was invented by Francesco Cirillo. If you are someone who has issues with multitasking, missed deadlines or even burnout, this method could be a good fit. This technique uses a timer to break down work intervals.
    4. How it works:
    1. Choose a task that you want to get done
    2. Set a timer for 25 minutes (or however long you want)
    3. Work on that task until your alarm sound
    4. Take a five minute break
    5. Repeat this process four times
    6. Take a longer break after the 4th pomodoro
  4. Time blocking: This is a strategy that involves being aware of how you spend your time during the day.
    5. How it works:
    1. Divide each 24-hour day into small blocks of time
    2. Estimate the amount of time that is required to each of the tasks and assign them into the time blocks in a planner or calendar.
    3. Add breaks between the start and the end of each tasks
    4. Work your way through the daily schedule
    5. You can adjust your schedule accordingly if certain tasks require more or less time.

STEP 3: Plan out your time like a pro 📅
Planning your time doesn't mean overwhelming your schedule with endless tasks. It's about setting a clear system up. When you have a clear plan, you stop panicking at the last minute, avoid unnecessary stress and you'll go from “I don't have enough time” to “Wow, I still have time to relax.”

  1. Create a calendar: This serves as your personal life GPS. You can keep track of your schedule and deadlines ahead of time. This is like having a personal assistant that reminds you about something important. Keep the calendar you created in a place where you can see it everyday, such as on top of your table beside your desk or it can be on your wall. But if you prefer a digital calendar, the first thing you need to do is to check it.
  2. Set reminders and deadlines: After creating your calendar, you need to give yourself periodic reminders to be able to stay on track. It is important that you set and know deadlines. You can do these by setting an alarm on your phone or by writing it down.
  3. Use a to-do list: This is one of the easy ways to prioritize. You can create a different to do list dedicated for different things such as daily to do or school to do. Write down the things you need to accomplish.
  4. Find tools that work best for you: Different people focus in different ways. The tools that you use to keep your schedule organized must be the right fit for you. You can do it digitally or by having physical planners.
  5. Break down tasks into small pieces: A task can seem so big that we dread doing it or we'll feel lost about how to start. All you have to do is divide it into mini-tasks. For example, instead of writing down a task “write and essay”, try “come up with outline ideas”, then “write introduction”. Small progress is progress. By doing this you can make something that may seem big into something manageable.

STEP 4: Work smarter everyday! 🧠

  1. Trim your to do list: Do not overwhelm yourself with 10+ tasks. You only have 24 hours in a day and if you find yourself having a long list, then things might drop altogether. Remove anything that doesn't need to be done that day or that week. By doing these it can help you to make a list that is less overwhelming. Do this the day before so you are ready to focus and get started the next morning.
  2. Avoid multitasking: Focus on one task at a time. Working with multiple tasks can be overwhelming and can result in mental overload. Doing too many tasks at the same time can affect your cognitive ability, which results in feeling unproductive or dissatisfied with the progress you make.
  3. Eliminate distractions: Study in a quiet place. Make sure to limit distractions when you are working on completing a task. Put your phone away, and make sure that you're setting yourself up to succeeding in completing tasks.
  4. Manage environment: Ensure that your workspace is comfortable and helps you to focus while you work. Your environment plays a huge role in your focus and productivity.

STEP 5: Stay disciplined and focused 🎯

  1. Use rewards and incentives: Just like the Pomodoro technique which allows you to reward yourself with a break, you can also use other rewards as a motivation. This is a great way for you to stay on track. It's much easier to focus on your tasks when you know that you have a reward to look forward to. Schedule activities that you enjoy as “rewards” for achieving your goals.
  2. Be realistic and flexible: There are times when things won't go as planned, instead of giving up you just have to adjust. Your goal is to make progress, not perfection. Be patient with yourself when the things you planned out don't go exactly as planned.
  3. Make time for yourself: Overworking yourself can lead to burnout. You have to ensure that you allocate time for yourself and your mental wellbeing. This helps maintain your work-life balance. Taking breaks doesn’t mean wasting time, it's about recharging your energy so you can work better.
  4. Ask for help when needed: Don't be afraid to turn to other people for help. This is one of the smartest things you can do instead of struggling alone. By reaching out to those around you, it can reduce the stress and you'll be able to gain knowledge and help you learn faster.

They help us to manage our stress levels, deal with anxiety and depression, and cope with difficult life events.

Check out these coping strategies:

  1. Relaxation Techniques
    1. Slow breathing - Stress can cause you to take fast, shallow breaths. Slowing down your breathing may help you to feel calm and less tense. Count to 3 as you slowly inhale, and then count to 6 as you exhale. Counting longer as you breathe out helps calm your nervous system.
    2. Meditation and Yoga - Engage in activities that promote present moment awareness, like mindfulness meditation or yoga.
    3. Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR)- The exercise involves tensing and releasing muscles, progressing throughout the body, with the focus on the release of the muscle as the relaxation phase.
  2. Problem Solving
    1. Identify the root cause of your stress

      How to identify the root cause of stress?
      A stress journal can help you identify regular stressors in your life and the ways you respond to them. Each time you feel stressed, jot it down in your journal or use a stress-tracking app. By keeping a daily log, you'll begin to notice patterns and common themes. Record the following:

      • What caused your stress (even if you have to guess)
      • How you felt physically and emotionally
      • How you responded to the stress
      • What actions you took to feel better

    2. Manage your time effectively

      How to manage time effectively?

      • Don't over-commit yourself: Avoid scheduling back-to-back activities. It's easy to underestimate how long tasks will take.
      • Prioritize tasks: List tasks by importance and tackle high-priority ones first. Complete stressful tasks early to make the rest of your day easier.
      • Break projects into steps: If a project feels overwhelming, break it down into smaller, manageable steps and focus on one at a time.
      • Delegate responsibility: Don't try to do everything yourself. Let others help when possible, and release the need to control every detail.

      • Be honest and open. It can sometimes feel uncomfortable sharing something so personal, but explaining how your feelings are affecting your life may help others to understand.
    2. Engage in activities that make you feel connected
      • Attend local events
      • Join clubs or groups
      • Attend workshops or classes
      • Participate in community clean-up events

Note Taking Strategies

Note-taking is necessary in studying, especially during discussion. Writing everything down on paper is not considered as a note-taking technique, which is one of the most common misconceptions about note-taking. A good note-taking strategy only involves important concepts and key information.

Here are some tips on how to properly do note-taking:

  • Prepare before the discussion. Make sure you have the materials you need for note taking.
  • If possible you can do advance reading of the topic that will be discussed.
  • Pay attention to the class. Listen carefully and think critically
  • Don't write down everything that is being said. Select only the concept that are important

Here are several methods that you can use to create an effective note-taking:

  1. Cornell Note-Taking is one of the popular note taking methods. It promotes organization, and comprehension of the overall material. For this method, a format must be followed where the paper must be divided into 4 sections. The 1st block at the top is where you will put the title, the middle of the paper is divided into two where the left side is usually smaller than the right side. The left side contains the keywords, and the right side contains your main notes and key thoughts. The remaining space on the bottom is for the summarization of your whole notes.
  2. Outline Note-Taking is used to breakdown broad ideas into specific and smaller one. It promotes structured and easy to follow notes. Aside from that, it is also easy to create, there is no format or template that needs to be followed. To use this method:
    • Identify the main idea
    • Add some sub-topics that are related or important to your main idea
    • You can add supporting ideas under the sub-topic to add more information
  3. Digital Note-Taking Approach is a technique that allows you to take your notes electronically. Some digital note taking is done by simply typing notes, while some devices or software allows users to make handwritten notes.

How to deal with academic burnout?

Academic burnout is a negative emotional, physical, and mental response to prolonged periods of studying, often leaving students feeling exhausted, frustrated, unmotivated, and less capable in the classroom. It typically results from years of continuous academic pressure or from intense weeks or months of study.

Addressing academic burnout is very important and cannot be overstated. Aside from affecting a student's performance and engagement, burnout can also have long-term impacts on mental health and overall quality of life. If left unaddressed, it may lead to anxiety, depression, and a significant decline in academic performance, potentially triggering a vicious cycle that puts a student’s academic journey at risk. That’s why recognizing the signs of burnout and implementing effective prevention strategies is essential. Building resilience and fostering healthy coping mechanisms can help students not only adapt to but thrive within the demands of academic life.

STEP 1: Give yourself time to rest
For both general wellbeing and successful learning, sleep is essential. Your brain can assimilate and consolidate knowledge when you take breaks from studying, which lessens mental exhaustion and avoids burnout. Making rest a priority will improve focus and retention, when will ultimately result in more fruitful study sessions, whether it takes the form of brief breaks or lengthier downtime. Understanding the value of sleep helps you establish a long lasting study schedule achievement and individual well-being.

STEP 2: Re-evaluate your study techniques + Plan your breaks
Maximizing efficacy and efficiency requires evaluating and improving your study methods. Think about the strategies that have previously worked for you and pinpoint any areas that need improvement. Use active learning techniques including employing visual aids, teaching others, and summarizing facts. Furthermore, it's critical to schedule regular breaks because research indicates that doing so might enhance focus and memory of material. Effective study techniques and well planned breaks can be used to produce a well-rounded strategy that improves learning results.

STEP 3: Focus on your progress and journey
It's simple to get obsessed with grades or results, but changing your viewpoint to value your development and educational path will help you have a more optimistic outlook. Recognize the little triumphs and advancements you make along the path since they add to your total development. By placing a strong emphasis on the learning process, you can become more resilient and motivated and see obstacles as chances for personal growth. You develop a greater respect for learning and individual accomplishments by appreciating your journey rather than concentrating just on the final product.

STEP 4: Learning is a process, stay kind to yourself and others

  2. Creating a budget document that outlines your expected monthly income and expenses
  3. Tracking your actual income and spending
  4. Making adjustments as needed to stay on track

50/30/20 Rule
The 50/30/20 rule is a simple budgeting strategy that can help you manage your finances effectively. It suggests allocating your after-tax income into three categories: 50% for needs, 30% for wants, and 20% for savings. By following this rule, you can ensure you're prioritizing essential expenses, controlling discretionary spending, and building a strong financial foundation for the future.

50% Needs
Needs are the essential expenses you must pay for survival and daily living. This includes things you can’t live without, such as rent, utilities, and basic necessities. For college students, this might include:

  1. Rent or dorm fees
  2. Utilities (if not included in rent)
  3. Groceries
  4. Insurance and Healthcare
  5. Minimum debt payments, such as student loans or credit card payments
If you're spending more than 50% of your income on needs, consider adjusting your lifestyle. You might downsize to a more affordable living situation, limit utility usage, or choose cheaper grocery options.

30% Wants
Wants are things you spend money on that aren't essential for survival but make life more enjoyable. For college students, this might include:

  1. Dining out, snacks, or coffee from cafes
  2. Concert tickets, movies, or other entertainment
  3. Non-essential clothing or accessories
  4. Travel for vacations or weekend trips
  5. Upgrading to the latest phone, gaming console, or laptop
Wants are the fun things that add enjoyment to life but can be adjusted depending on your financial situation. If you're on a tight budget, consider cutting back on some of these non-essential purchases.

20% Savings
It's important to set aside 20% of your income for savings and investments, even as a student. While it may be challenging, building up an emergency fund is essential in case of unexpected situations like losing a part-time job or dealing with a medical emergency. Here are some ways to save as a student:

  1. Build an emergency fund for unexpected expenses
  2. Contribute to a retirement account like an IRA (if possible)
  3. Set aside money for future big purchases (e.g., a car, travel, or housing after graduation)
  4. Pay off any high-interest debt (such as credit card debt)
By following the 50/30/20 rule, you can create a balanced approach to managing your finances, even on a student budget. It helps you stay on track with your essential needs, enjoy life, and save for your future!

Understanding Loans

Loans come in various forms, each designed to meet specific financial needs. Understanding the different types of loans can help you choose the right one for your situation. Here are the main categories:

  1. Student Loans
    These loans are specifically designed for students to help cover the cost of tuition, books, and other education-related expenses. They usually offer lower interest rates and more flexible repayment options compared to other types of loans. Student loans can be federal (offered by the government) or private (offered by banks and financial institutions)
  2. Personal Loans
    Personal loans are unsecured loans, meaning they don’t require collateral. They can be used for various purposes, such as consolidating debt, funding home improvements, or covering medical expenses. Interest rates on personal loans can vary depending on your creditworthiness.
  3. Mortgagae Loans
    A mortgage is a loan specifically used to purchase real estate. The property itself serves as collateral, meaning the lender can take ownership of the property if the borrower fails to repay the loan. Mortgage loans typically have long repayment terms (15 to 30 years) and can have fixed or adjustable interest rates.
  4. Auto Loans
    An auto loan is used to finance the purchase of a car. Like a mortgage, the vehicle acts as collateral, meaning if the borrower fails to make payments, the lender can repossess the car. Auto loans typically have shorter repayment terms (3 to 7 years) and lower interest rates compared to personal loans.
  5. Credit Card Loans
    Credit cards are a type of revolving credit where you can borrow money up to a set limit. The loan is repaid through monthly payments, and if the balance isn’t paid off in full, interest is charged. Credit cards are generally an expensive way to borrow money due to high-interest rates.
  6. Home Equity Loans
    A home equity loan allows you to borrow money using the equity (the value of the home minus what is owed on it) in your property. This is a secured loan, meaning your home is at risk if you fail to repay. Home equity loans typically have lower interest rates than personal loans because the property serves as collateral.
  7. Payday Loans
    Payday loans are short-term, high-interest loans designed to cover urgent, small expenses until your next paycheck. These loans are typically due in full by your next payday, and failure to repay on time can lead to significant fees and a cycle of debt.
  8. Business Loans
    Business loans are used to fund the startup, expansion, or operation of a business. These loans can be secured (requiring collateral) or unsecured and typically come with higher loan amounts and longer repayment terms.

Importance of paying off interest early
Paying off interest early is crucial for several reasons, as it can significantly impact your overall financial health.

  1. Reduces the Total Amount Paid
    When you pay off interest early, you're reducing the amount of interest that accrues on your loan or credit balance. Interest compounds over time, which means that if you only make minimum payments, you'll pay much more in interest in the long run. By paying early, you minimize the interest on the principal, which results in less total debt.
  2. Helps you Pay Off Debt faster
    By making early interest payments, you free up more of your monthly payment to go toward reducing the principal. This accelerates the process of paying off the loan. The faster you pay off the principal, the less interest you’ll owe, helping you achieve financial freedom sooner.
  3. Improves Credit Score
    Reducing your debt more quickly, including paying off interest, can positively impact your credit score. Lowering the total amount you owe improves your credit utilization ratio (the amount of credit you're using compared to your total available credit), which can help raise your credit score.
  4. Prevents the Debt from Snowballing
    Interest can quickly cause debt to grow if you're not paying it off. When you allow interest to accumulate without making extra payments, the balance can snowball, making it even harder to pay off over time. Paying it off early ensures the debt doesn’t increase beyond what you can manage.
  5. More Financial Flexibility
    The less money you owe in interest, the more financial flexibility you have. Paying off interest early can free up cash flow that you can put toward other savings, investments, or expenses. It gives you more control over your finances and reduces financial stress.
  6. Saves Money in the Long Run
    The most significant benefit of paying off interest early is the amount of money you save. By reducing the interest you owe, you’ll spend less over the life of the loan, which can be a significant amount depending on the interest rate and loan duration.

CPR or Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation is an emergency lifesaving procedure performed when the heart stops beating. Immediate CPR can double or triple chances of survival after cardiac arrest.

CPR should be used when you see someone who is unresponsive and is not breathing or only gasping. Having more bystanders trained in this simple skill can help save lives by putting more cardiac arrest victims within a few steps of lifesaving assistance.

Types of CPR

  • Hands-Only CPR: Hands-only CPR is an easy-to-learn skill that could save a life. It involves calling 9-1-1, sending someone for the AED if available and then giving continuous chest compressions. It only takes minutes to learn.
  • Full CPR with Rescue Breaths: While Hands-only CPR can be lifesaving, learning full CPR is still very important. Getting trained in full CPR – combinations of chest compressions and rescue breaths – will increase your confidence and may enable you to help in other types of emergencies. Full CPR is ideal for all ages, and especially for people who are more likely to experience respiratory emergencies such as children and infants.

How do you perform CPR?
Always remember C-A-B: Compressions, Airway, and Breathing

  1. Compressions
    • Lay the person flat on their back on a firm surface.
    • Place your hands in the center of the chest, one on top of the other.
    • Keep your elbows straight and your shoulders over your hands.
    • Push hard and fast, at least 2 inches deep, 100-120 compressions per minute (about the beat of "Stayin' Alive").
    • Let the chest rise between compressions.
  2. Airway - After 30 compressions, tilt the head back and lift the chin to open the airway.
  3. Breathing - Pinch the nostrils shut and give two rescue breaths. Seal your mouth over theirs and breathe in until the chest rises. If the chest doesn’t rise, adjust the head and try again.

HANDLING EMERGENCY SITUATIONS (Fire Drills & Medical Emergencies)

Emergencies can be stressful, but knowing what to do can help you stay calm and take action. Here’s how to handle things like fire drills and medical emergencies with confidence.

  1. FIRE DRILLS

    The purpose of a fire drill is to familiarize and reinforce proper evacuation routes and practices. The goal is to have the proper actions be an automatic response whenever fire alarms sound, so that everyone safely evacuates the area in an orderly manner. Fire drills are required annually for most facilities, and should be used to evaluate warning features and occupant knowledge.

    Steps to Conduct a Successful Fire Drill
    1. Plan the Fire Drill
      • Set Clear Objectives: Make sure the drill helps everyone evacuate quickly and safely, tests the fire alarm system, checks emergency exits, and assesses the response of safety officers.
      • Schedule Wisely: Choose a time that avoids peak hours to ensure maximum participation without disrupting normal activities.
      • Notify Key Personnel: Ensure fire wardens, safety officers, and local emergency services are informed ahead of time so they can be ready.
    2. Communicate with Building Occupants
      • Give Advance Notice: Let everyone know about the scheduled drill so they can be prepared.
      • Share Important Info: Distribute evacuation maps, instructions, and training videos to make sure everyone understands their role.
    3. During the Fire Drill
      • Activate the Alarm: Make sure the fire alarm is loud enough for everyone to hear.
      • Observe the Evacuation: Watch how quickly everyone evacuates, check if there are any issues with the exits, and ensure everyone follows the procedures.
      • Assist Others: Fire wardens should help guide people, especially those with mobility issues, to the assembly area in a calm and orderly way.
    4. Post-Drill Review
      • Gather Feedback: After the drill, ask for input from participants and fire wardens to see what worked and what could be improved.
      • Evaluate Performance: Look at how quickly people evacuated and if everyone followed the plan properly.
      • Update Plans: Use the feedback to make any necessary improvements for future drills and update the emergency evacuation plan.
  2. MEDICAL EMERGENCIES

    When a medical emergency strikes, every second counts. Knowing how to respond quickly and calmly can help save a life in a crisis.
    Before an emergency happens, know the quickest route to the nearest emergency department and keep emergency numbers posted at home and saved in your phone. Everyone should know how to call these numbers. Also, know which hospital your healthcare provider uses, and wear a medical ID if you have a chronic condition. Older adults, especially those living alone, should consider a personal emergency response system.

    In an emergency:
    1. Stay calm and call 911 or the local emergency number.
    2. If needed, begin CPR or rescue breathing (if trained).
    3. Place unconscious people in the recovery position, unless there’s a neck injury.
    At the emergency room, the most serious conditions are treated first. Call 911 if:
    • The person’s condition is life-threatening.
    • Their condition could worsen on the way to the hospital.
    • Moving them could cause further injury.
    • Paramedic skills or equipment are needed.
    • Traffic or distance might delay getting to the hospital.

HOW TO CREATE A DAILY ROUTINE?

  1. Make a list of your daily activities.
    Start with the things that you already do every day. Then add things that you want to do every day. Think of how the things you already do can trigger you to do the things you want to do. This will help you put the pieces of your routine together.
  2. Set a goal for your routine.
    The ultimate goal you want to achieve helps you decide which tasks to include in your routine. Do you want your routine to help you become more productive? To get fit? To live a healthier life? Once you know your routine's purpose, you'll know how to arrange your day to satisfy that purpose.
  3. Break tasks into smaller chunks that are easier to achieve
    Breaking up large tasks into manageable chunks helps you get more done. Take complex tasks and list their components by breaking these up, you can get a lot done without even thinking about it.
  4. Determine the time you need to complete the tasks in your routine.
    Each of the individual tasks in your routine will take you an average amount of time. In general, try to give each task just a little longer than it actually takes you to complete. Add those padded estimates together to figure out how long your entire routine should take. That way, you'll know when you can get it done without rushing or feeling stressed.
  5. Start your routine at the same time every day.
    Choose a distinct block of time for daily routines, such as your morning routine and your bedtime routine. Designating these specific times for your routine helps you structure your day and make sure you have time to do the things you need to do.
  6. Add less frequent tasks to your routine gradually.
    You likely have some tasks that you need to do but you don't need to do them every day. Make a list of tasks that you need to do weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly. Then, you can add those into your routines where you have time and it makes sense to do so.
  7. Follow your routine for at least 2 months to make it a habit.
    It does take some time to turn a routine into a habit so that you can reap all of the benefits. Once the different parts of your routine become habitual, you'll do them without thinking and that takes a lot of stress and pressure off your mind.
  8. Forgive yourself when you slip up.
    Making mistakes is just par for the course when you're trying to engrain a new habit into your daily routine. While it's possible that you'll take to it right away, remember that change isn't linear. If you believe that your routine is good for you and is worth doing, you'll ultimately get it down.
  9. Keep track of your progress to gauge whether your routine is working.
    At the end of each week, look back at how well you've followed your routine and whether you're making progress towards your goals. If you're having a harder time with some parts of your routine than others, figure out why.
  10. Make changes to your routine as needed.
    After your routine has become habitual, it's easy to feel stuck in it. If you become overly rigid about your routine, you'll keep doing it long after it's stopped actually serving your needs. Treat your routine as fluid and subject to change, rather than as something that's set in stone.
